![](https://image.cnbcfm.com/api/v1/image/108236160-1772735114407-108236160-1764789658120-gettyimages-2249797033-mms21052_jel7caql.jpeg?v=1772747128&w=1920&h=1080)

The Pentagon formally designated Anthropic a “supply chain risk,” ordering defense vendors and contractors to certify they are not using Claude in DoD work and triggering an immediate wind-down of Pentagon use of Anthropic’s models. The dispute centers on contract terms: Anthropic sought explicit red lines against mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons, while the DoD insisted on access for “all lawful purposes.” Anthropic says the designation under 10 USC 3252 is narrow, applies only to uses tied directly to DoD contracts, and violates the statute’s “least restrictive means” requirement; the company plans to challenge it in court. At the same time, multiple reports indicate Claude has supported U.S. military operations in Iran for intelligence analysis, modeling and simulation, operational planning, and cyber operations.

After Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth suggested no military partner may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic, confusion spread about the scope. Cloud providers disputed that interpretation; Microsoft, Google, and AWS clarified Claude remains available for non-defense workloads via M365, GitHub, AI Foundry, and Google Cloud. Meanwhile, OpenAI announced a DoD deal to deploy its models in classified environments, citing prohibitions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapon systems. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei called OpenAI’s framing “straight up lies” and “safety theater,” arguing the agreement still hinges on “all lawful purposes.” Public backlash followed, with reports of a surge in ChatGPT uninstalls (+295%) and Claude rising to the top of the App Store. Anthropic says it is working to “deescalate” with the Pentagon and will support warfighters during the transition at nominal cost.

![](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/d3RzMsGpZ7SwkUK7ez58Qa-2560-80.jpg)

OpenAI signed a Pentagon (Department of War) agreement after Anthropic walked away over two explicit red lines: no mass domestic surveillance and no fully autonomous weapons. OpenAI says its deal enforces “red lines” via legal references and technical guardrails, citing compliance with the Fourth Amendment, FISA, EO 12333, and DoD policy, plus cloud-only deployment, security clearances, and classifiers to monitor use. Critics counter that the terms still hinge on “any lawful use,” a standard that has historically enabled bulk data programs like PRISM and Verizon call record collection, and they note vague carve-outs such as prohibitions on “unconstrained” or “generalized” monitoring. OpenAI later posted revised language that “the AI system shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons,” and said defense intelligence components (e.g., NSA) are excluded, but it has not released the full contract; legal experts warn loopholes remain and that policy reinterpretations can broaden surveillance.

The fallout was swift: a “QuitGPT” boycott, in-person protests at OpenAI’s SF HQ with “No killer robots” messaging, and a 295% surge in ChatGPT uninstalls. Anthropic’s Claude climbed to No. 1 in the U.S. App Store, with daily sign-ups breaking records, free users up 60% since January, and paid subscribers more than doubling this year. Inside OpenAI, hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski resigned, citing rushed governance and insufficiently defined guardrails on surveillance and lethal autonomy. During the same period, the Pentagon labeled Anthropic a supply-chain risk after it refused “any lawful purpose,” officials affirmed that OpenAI’s deal flows from “all lawful use,” and experts argued classifiers cannot verify human-in-the-loop for lethal decisions or distinguish one-off queries from mass-surveillance programs. OpenAI says it asked DoD to offer its terms to all AI vendors.

#### [OpenAI launches GPT-5.4 with Pro and Thinking versions](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/05/openai-launches-gpt-5-4-with-pro-and-thinking-versions/)
Related:
 * [‘We heard your feedback loud and clear’ — OpenAI introduces new ChatGPT 5.3 Instant to ‘reduce the cringe’ for all users](https://www.techradar.com/ai-platforms-assistants/chatgpt/we-heard-your-feedback-loud-and-clear-openai-introduces-new-chatgpt-5-3-instant-to-reduce-the-cringe-for-all-users)

![](https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/5.4-Thinking_Hero-SEO.png?resize=1200,675)

OpenAI released GPT-5.4 in three variants—standard, Pro, and Thinking—positioning it as a more capable and efficient frontier model for professional work. The API now supports up to a 1 million-token context window, and improved token efficiency means it can solve the same tasks with fewer tokens than prior models. GPT-5.4 posted record results on OSWorld-Verified and WebArena Verified for computer use, scored 83% on OpenAI’s GDPval for knowledge work, and led Mercor’s APEX-Agents benchmark for law and finance tasks. OpenAI also introduced Tool Search for API tool calling, allowing the model to fetch tool definitions on demand to reduce token overhead and latency, and reported fewer factual errors versus GPT-5.2 (33% fewer erroneous claims; 18% fewer responses with errors). A new safety evaluation targeting chain-of-thought indicates the GPT-5.4 Thinking model is less prone to deceptive reasoning traces, supporting CoT monitoring as an effective control.

Separately, OpenAI upgraded ChatGPT’s default to GPT-5.3 Instant to “reduce the cringe,” tuning away unnecessary refusals and long safety disclaimers while keeping guardrails. GPT-5.3 Instant focuses on direct answers for reasonable requests and improves accuracy, with ~27% fewer hallucinations during online research and ~20% fewer without browsing, plus better blending of web data with prior knowledge. The change updates the baseline chat experience across common uses while retaining GPT-5.2 Instant for paid users as a legacy option. Together with GPT-5.4’s pro-focused gains—large context windows, Tool Search efficiency, and top benchmark scores—OpenAI is pushing both high-end professional capability and everyday conversational quality.

[Exploratory Memory-Augmented LLM Agent via Hybrid On- and Off-Policy Optimization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23008). This work jointly updates an LLM’s parametric policy with reinforcement learning and its non‑parametric external memory via interaction using a hybrid on‑ and off‑policy rollout scheme (EMPO2), improving exploration and delivering substantial gains on ScienceWorld and WebShop.

[HyperSteer: Activation Steering at Scale with Hypernetworks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03292). The authors introduce HyperSteer—trainable hypernetworks that generate steering vectors for an instruction‑tuned language model conditioned on steering prompts (and optionally model context)—achieving better performance than prior activation‑steering methods and matching steering‑via‑prompting while scaling logarithmically with training prompt count.

[A Very Big Video Reasoning Suite](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20159). A community‑built corpus of over 1 million video clips and 2 million images, plus a rule‑based, human‑aligned evaluation suite, enables standardized benchmarking and a large‑scale scaling study that reveals persistent gaps between current models and human‑level video reasoning.

[Robometer: Scaling General-Purpose Robotic Reward Models via Trajectory Comparisons](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.02115). This approach trains a video‑and‑language dense reward model using both frame‑level progress on expert demos and pairwise trajectory preference supervision—leveraging a 1M+ trajectory RBM‑1M dataset with many failed/suboptimal runs and augmentations—to better scale and generalize rewards for downstream RL, imitation, and failure detection.

[Agentic Code Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.01896). The paper shows how prompting agents with structured, semi‑formal certificate templates—requiring explicit premises, traced code paths, and per‑test conclusions—improves execution‑free verification of patch equivalence, code QA, and fault localization across diverse repositories.

[Beyond Language Modeling: An Exploration of Multimodal Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03276). Training a single model from scratch with shared next‑token and diffusion objectives across text, images, and video, the authors show that semantic visual representations (RAE), careful data mixes, and Mixture‑of‑Experts architectures enable joint vision‑language pretraining with minimal modality interference, improved world‑modeling abilities, and distinct scaling behaviors favoring sparse MoE.

[Semantic Tube Prediction: Beating LLM Data Efficiency with JEPA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22617). By adding a JEPA‑inspired auxiliary loss that minimizes components of hidden‑state differences perpendicular to inferred geodesic trajectories, the Semantic Tube objective separates noise from semantic signal to improve SNR, data efficiency, and diversity in autoregressive model training.

[Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence](https://www.anthropic.com/research/labor-market-impacts). This analysis combines measures of theoretical LLM capability with observed workplace usage to create an “observed exposure” metric and finds early evidence of limited impact on unemployment but some slowdown in hiring for young workers in highly exposed occupations.

[The Spike, the Sparse and the Sink: Anatomy of Massive Activations and Attention Sinks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05498). The study shows that massive outlier activations and attention sinks arise from specific architectural choices—especially normalization and attention dimensionality—and can be independently mitigated without harming language‑model performance.

[Progressive Residual Warmup for Language Model Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05369). This method initializes per‑layer residual scaling at zero and gradually increases deeper layers’ residual contributions more slowly than shallow ones during warmup, prioritizing early‑layer learning to reduce interference and improve perplexity across model scales.

[Reasoning Models Struggle to Control their Chains of Thought](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05706). Tests on 14,076 problems show models generally fail to follow explicit instructions to suppress, add, or stylistically modify their chains of thought, with controllability varying by model size, training stage, task difficulty, and prompting.
