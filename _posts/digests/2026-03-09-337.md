#### [OpenAI launches GPT-5.4 with Pro and Thinking versions](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/05/openai-launches-gpt-5-4-with-pro-and-thinking-versions/)
Related:
 * [‘We heard your feedback loud and clear’ — OpenAI introduces new ChatGPT 5.3 Instant to ‘reduce the cringe’ for all users](https://www.techradar.com/ai-platforms-assistants/chatgpt/we-heard-your-feedback-loud-and-clear-openai-introduces-new-chatgpt-5-3-instant-to-reduce-the-cringe-for-all-users)

![](https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/5.4-Thinking_Hero-SEO.png?resize=1200,675)

OpenAI introduced GPT-5.4 as its new frontier model with three variants: the standard model, GPT-5.4 Pro (optimized for high performance), and GPT-5.4 Thinking (a reasoning model). The API now supports context windows up to 1 million tokens and delivers improved token efficiency, completing the same tasks with fewer tokens than GPT-5.2. On benchmarks, GPT-5.4 posted record results on OSWorld-Verified and WebArena Verified for computer use, reached 83% on OpenAI’s GDPval for knowledge work, and led Mercor’s APEX-Agents for legal and finance tasks, while claiming faster, cheaper performance for long-horizon deliverables like slide decks, financial models, and legal analysis. OpenAI also reports fewer hallucinations and factual errors—33% fewer incorrect individual claims and 18% fewer erroneous responses than GPT-5.2—and introduced Tool Search for API tool-calling to dynamically fetch tool definitions, cutting token overhead and latency in tool-rich systems.

ChatGPT’s default has been updated to GPT-5.3 Instant to “reduce the cringe,” with tuning to avoid unnecessary refusals and shorten safety disclaimers while maintaining guardrails. According to the company, GPT-5.3 Instant reduces hallucinations during online research by about 27% and offline by 20%, and better blends web data with prior knowledge. Alongside a new safety evaluation suggesting the Thinking model is less likely to misrepresent chain-of-thought—with findings that support CoT monitoring as a safety tool—the rollout aims to boost both reliability and usability. GPT-5.3 Instant replaces 5.2 as the baseline chat experience (with 5.2 Instant still available for paid users), while GPT-5.4 targets enterprise-grade performance, large contexts, stronger reasoning, and lower cost per task.

#### [Anthropic officially told by DOD that it's a supply chain risk even as Claude used in Iran](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/05/anthropic-pentagon-ai-claude-iran.html)

 * ['No ethics at all': the 'cancel ChatGPT' trend is growing after OpenAI signs a deal with the US military](https://www.techradar.com/ai-platforms-assistants/chatgpt/no-ethics-at-all-the-cancel-chatgpt-trend-is-growing-after-openai-signs-a-deal-with-the-us-military)

![](https://image.cnbcfm.com/api/v1/image/108236160-1772735114407-108236160-1764789658120-gettyimages-2249797033-mms21052_jel7caql.jpeg?v=1772747128&w=1920&h=1080)

The Pentagon has formally designated Anthropic a “supply-chain risk,” an unprecedented move for a U.S. company that immediately requires Defense Department vendors to certify they aren’t using Claude in DoD work. The dispute centers on Anthropic’s demand for explicit guardrails against “mass domestic surveillance” and “fully autonomous weapons,” which conflicted with the DoD’s requirement for access to AI “for all lawful purposes.” Even as talks broke down, U.S. officials confirmed Claude had already been used to support operations in Iran, including intelligence analysis, modeling and simulation, operational planning, and cyber operations. Anthropic argues 10 USC 3252 is narrow and restricts Claude’s use only when it is part of a direct DoD contract, pledges to challenge the designation in court, and promises nominal-cost access to ease any transition off Pentagon systems.

Public messaging compounded confusion. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted that no DoD contractor could do business with Anthropic “effective immediately,” while Microsoft, Google, and AWS clarified Claude remains available for non-defense workloads through M365, GitHub, AI Foundry, and Google Cloud. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei called the designation “retaliatory and punitive,” yet told investors the company is seeking to “deescalate” and reach an agreement, reiterating its two red lines and that Anthropic does not make operational decisions. OpenAI, by contrast, announced a DoD deal to deploy its models in classified environments under “all lawful purposes,” with asserted carve-outs for mass surveillance and autonomous weapon systems—claims Amodei privately labeled “straight up lies” and “safety theater.” Backlash followed: reports cite a surge in ChatGPT uninstalls (+295%) and Claude climbing the App Store charts, while experts note that any “lawful use” guardrails hinge on current law and policy that could change.

![](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/d3RzMsGpZ7SwkUK7ez58Qa-2560-80.jpg)

OpenAI signed a Pentagon/Department of War agreement after Anthropic rejected similar terms over two red lines: no mass domestic surveillance and no fully autonomous weapons. OpenAI says its deal embeds guardrails and “red lines,” citing prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance, human responsibility for use of force, cloud-only deployment, classifiers to monitor misuse, excluded “defense intelligence components” (e.g., NSA), and a clause stating systems “shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons and nationals.” Reporting, however, indicates the contract hinges on “any lawful use,” tying constraints to authorities like the Fourth Amendment, the National Security Act, FISA, and EO 12333—legal frameworks historically interpreted to permit bulk collection programs such as PRISM and telephony metadata. Critics point to vague qualifiers (“unconstrained,” “open-ended,” “generalized”) and note that OpenAI’s lethal-autonomy language mirrors DoD’s 2023 directive—allowing AI to support the kill chain so long as policy requires human control—falling short of Anthropic’s stricter demand to ban unsupervised lethal autonomous weapons.

Reaction was swift. The “QuitGPT” movement spread on social media and to the streets, with protesters outside OpenAI HQ decrying surveillance and “killer robots,” and Reddit guides circulating on data export and account deletion. App metrics flipped: Anthropic’s Claude climbed from outside the top 100 in late January to No. 1 in the U.S. App Store, daily sign-ups hit records, free users rose 60% since January, and paid subscribers more than doubled; ChatGPT uninstalls reportedly surged 295%. The U.S. government labeled Anthropic a “supply-chain risk,” ordered agencies to stop using Claude, and threatened blacklisting—moves unusual for a domestic company—while OpenAI amended its public terms and said DoW intelligence agencies are excluded. Legal and policy experts remain skeptical without the full contract text, warning that “any lawful use” and secret reinterpretations could still enable AI-assisted domestic surveillance using commercially acquired data and large-scale pattern analysis. Even an OpenAI hardware executive, Caitlin Kalinowski, resigned, citing rushed governance and insufficiently defined guardrails.

[Exploratory Memory-Augmented LLM Agent via Hybrid On- and Off-Policy Optimization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23008). This method trains LLM-based agents by jointly updating policy parameters with RL and a non-parametric memory module using a hybrid on- and off-policy scheme, yielding substantially better exploration and task performance (e.g., large gains on ScienceWorld and modest gains on WebShop) plus improved generalization to unseen environments.

[HyperSteer: Activation Steering at Scale with Hypernetworks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03292). The authors use end-to-end trainable hypernetworks to generate task-specific activation steering vectors for an instruction-tuned base LM, outperforming prior activation-steering methods on AxBench and exhibiting logarithmic scaling with the number of steering prompts.

[A Very Big Video Reasoning Suite](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20159). The work introduces a 2-million-image/1-million-video training dataset, a verifiable evaluation benchmark with human-aligned rule-based scorers, and scaling experiments showing models improve but remain well below human performance, with distinct trade-offs among reasoning skills.

[Robometer: Scaling General-Purpose Robotic Reward Models via Trajectory Comparisons](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.02115). A video-and-language reward model is trained using both frame-level progress on expert data and pairwise trajectory preference supervision—leveraging a new RBM-1M dataset of 1M diverse trajectories (including failures and augmentations)—to improve reward calibration, generalization across embodiments and scenes, and downstream RL and failure-detection performance.

[Agentic Code Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.01896). The authors show how prompting agents with structured, semi-formal certificate templates forces explicit evidence and interprocedural traces, improving execution-free verification of patch equivalence, QA, and fault localization across benchmarks.

[Beyond Language Modeling: An Exploration of Multimodal Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03276). Training from scratch with unified next-token and diffusion objectives across diverse text, image, and video data, the study finds that semantic RAE visual representations, high-granularity MoE, and inclusion of video enable positive transfer to VQA, generation, and world-modeling tasks, while revealing a vision–language scaling asymmetry that MoE helps resolve.

[Semantic Tube Prediction: Beating LLM Data Efficiency with JEPA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22617). The proposed method adds a JEPA-inspired auxiliary loss that constrains hidden-state dynamics to a low-noise “semantic tube,” separating signal from perpendicular noise and improving data efficiency, generation quality, and diversity versus next-token prediction alone.

[Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence](https://www.anthropic.com/research/labor-market-impacts). This observed-exposure measure combines task-level theoretical LLM capability with real-world Claude usage to show that AI adoption remains well below technical potential; more-exposed occupations (e.g., programmers, customer service reps) are projected to grow less and have distinct demographic profiles; and so far unemployment hasn’t risen overall, though hiring of young workers into exposed jobs has slightly slowed.

[The Spike, the Sparse and the Sink: Anatomy of Massive Activations and Attention Sinks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05498). The paper shows that these phenomena co-occur in pretrained decoder-only Transformers due to specific normalization settings, attention-space dimensionality, and training context lengths—not because they are essential—and that each can be independently mitigated without harming model performance.

[Progressive Residual Warmup for Language Model Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05369). A training schedule gradually scales each layer’s residual contribution from zero to one—warming up deeper layers more slowly than shallow ones—to prioritize early-layer learning, reduce layer interference, and improve perplexity and stability across model sizes.

[Reasoning Models Struggle to Control their Chains of Thought](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05706). The authors introduce CoT-Control, a 14,076‑instance benchmark that programmatically measures models’ ability to follow explicit instructions to modify their chain-of-thought (suppression, addition, or stylistic changes), finding that most reasoning models—especially after reasoning-focused training—struggle to intentionally control their CoTs despite gains from larger model size or situational-awareness prompts.
