#### [Anthropic officially told by DOD that it's a supply chain risk even as Claude used in Iran](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/05/anthropic-pentagon-ai-claude-iran.html)

![](https://image.cnbcfm.com/api/v1/image/108236160-1772735114407-108236160-1764789658120-gettyimages-2249797033-mms21052_jel7caql.jpeg?v=1772747128&w=1920&h=1080)

The Pentagon formally designated Anthropic and its Claude models a “supply chain risk” under 10 USC 3252, directing defense vendors to certify they do not use Claude on DoD work and effectively blocking direct DoD use going forward. The move followed a breakdown in negotiations over two explicit Anthropic guardrails—no mass domestic surveillance and no support for fully autonomous weapons—versus the DoD’s demand for “all lawful uses” without carve‑outs. Meanwhile, multiple reports say Claude supported U.S. military operations in Iran for intelligence analysis, modeling/simulation, operational planning, and cyber operations. Anthropic maintains the designation is narrow and applies only to work under DoD contracts; Microsoft, Google, and AWS say Claude remains available for non‑defense workloads. Anthropic plans to challenge the decision in court and to support permitted transitions for warfighters at nominal cost.

Industry reactions diverged. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said no DoD contractor may conduct commercial activity with Anthropic, a claim Anthropic argues exceeds statutory authority and conflicts with 10 USC 3252’s “least restrictive means” requirement. OpenAI, by contrast, announced a DoD deal to deploy its models in classified environments, asserting prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and autonomous weapon systems while allowing “all lawful purposes”—which Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei called “straight up lies” and “safety theater.” Uncertainty remains among federal contracting experts about which downstream users must disengage, though Big Tech platforms indicate most enterprise customers are unaffected. Public backlash targeted OpenAI, with reports of a spike in ChatGPT uninstalls and Claude climbing App Store rankings, as debate continues over how enforceable “lawful use” clauses are and whether future legal shifts could undermine stated red lines.

![](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/d3RzMsGpZ7SwkUK7ez58Qa-2560-80.jpg)

OpenAI signed an agreement to deploy its models in Department of Defense classified environments days after Anthropic walked away from a Pentagon deal over two red lines: no mass domestic surveillance and no fully autonomous weapons. OpenAI says its contract enforces similar “red lines” through a multi‑layered safety stack—prohibitions on mass domestic surveillance, autonomous weapon systems, and high‑stakes automated decisions; cloud‑only deployment; retained control over guardrails; cleared OpenAI personnel in the loop; and contractual protections. Critics point to the contract’s “any lawful use” foundation and references to EO 12333, FISA, and other authorities as loopholes that have historically enabled bulk data collection, arguing OpenAI effectively accepted what Anthropic refused. Following backlash, OpenAI updated language to state “the AI system shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons,” and said defense intelligence components (e.g., NSA) are excluded—but it has not released the full contract, leaving legal experts and policy groups unconvinced about enforceability and scope.

The fallout was immediate and visible. Anthropic’s Claude reached No. 1 in Apple’s U.S. App Store, with daily sign‑ups setting records, free users up 60% since January, and paid subscribers more than doubling this year; ChatGPT uninstalls reportedly jumped 295% amid “QuitGPT” protests at OpenAI’s HQ. Reactions among public figures split: Sen. Ron Wyden warned that AI plus commercially acquired data could enable profiling of Americans; retired Gen. Paul Nakasone, now on OpenAI’s board, urged the Pentagon to partner with all leading LLM firms; and a Trump administration undersecretary said OpenAI accepted a compromise Anthropic rejected as DoD labeled Anthropic a “supply‑chain risk.” Internally, OpenAI hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski resigned, citing rushed governance and inadequate deliberation around surveillance and lethal autonomy. Skeptics also note that technical safeguards like classifiers and cloud‑only deployment can’t reliably verify a “human in the loop” for lethal force or distinguish one‑off queries from mass surveillance, particularly if such usage is deemed lawful under existing policy.

#### [OpenAI launches GPT-5.4 with Pro and Thinking versions](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/05/openai-launches-gpt-5-4-with-pro-and-thinking-versions/)
Related:
 * [‘We heard your feedback loud and clear’ — OpenAI introduces new ChatGPT 5.3 Instant to ‘reduce the cringe’ for all users](https://www.techradar.com/ai-platforms-assistants/chatgpt/we-heard-your-feedback-loud-and-clear-openai-introduces-new-chatgpt-5-3-instant-to-reduce-the-cringe-for-all-users)

![](https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/5.4-Thinking_Hero-SEO.png?resize=1200,675)

OpenAI released GPT‑5.4 in three variants—standard, Pro, and Thinking—positioning it as a frontier model for professional work with major efficiency and reasoning upgrades. The API now supports context windows up to 1 million tokens and introduces Tool Search, which fetches tool definitions on demand to cut token use and latency versus embedding all tools in system prompts. Reported benchmark gains include record results on OSWorld‑Verified and WebArena Verified for computer use, 83% on OpenAI’s GDPval for knowledge work, and top performance on Mercor’s APEX‑Agents for law and finance, with strong performance on long‑horizon deliverables like slide decks, financial models, and legal analysis. OpenAI says hallucinations are reduced versus GPT‑5.2 (33% fewer erroneous claims and 18% fewer responses with errors). A new safety evaluation also suggests GPT‑5.4 Thinking is less likely to misrepresent its chain‑of‑thought, supporting CoT monitoring as a viable safety tool.

In a separate update, OpenAI made GPT‑5.3 Instant the default ChatGPT model to “reduce the cringe” by trimming unnecessary refusals and shortening safety disclaimers while keeping guardrails in place. The model aims for more direct answers on reasonable requests and shows improved accuracy, with roughly 27% fewer hallucinations during online research and about 20% fewer offline, plus better fusion of web data with existing knowledge. GPT‑5.3 Instant replaces 5.2 as the baseline for most users, while legacy access remains for paid users. Together, these releases target both enterprise‑grade workloads (1M‑token context, Tool Search, agents/benchmarks) and everyday chat usability (fewer caveats, snappier tone), emphasizing token efficiency, faster/cheaper requests, and measurable reductions in hallucinations.

#### Research

[Exploratory Memory-Augmented LLM Agent via Hybrid On- and Off-Policy Optimization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23008). The method jointly trains model parameters with on‑ and off‑policy RL while updating a non‑parametric memory during interaction, using memory‑guided and memory‑free rollouts to drive broader exploration and improve performance on embodied reasoning benchmarks.

[HyperSteer: Activation Steering at Scale with Hypernetworks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03292). Hypernetworks generate task‑conditioned steering vectors for an instruction‑tuned base LM, achieving better and more scalable activation steering than prior methods and matching steering‑via‑prompting on held‑out prompts.

[A Very Big Video Reasoning Suite](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20159). VBVR‑Dataset (1,007,500 clips) spans 200 tasks across five cognitive pillars, paired with a verifiable VBVR‑Bench aligned with human judgments; a scaling study shows improved yet still sub‑human video reasoning as model and data scale increase.

[Robometer: Scaling General-Purpose Robotic Reward Models via Trajectory Comparisons](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.02115). A video‑language dense reward model is trained using both frame‑level progress on expert data and preference‑based trajectory comparisons (including many unlabeled failures), improving generalization and downstream RL/IL performance.

[Agentic Code Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.01896). Prompting LLM agents with semi‑formal structured certificates—explicit premises, traced code paths, and per‑test evidence—improves execution‑free patch equivalence verification, code QA, and fault localization across diverse benchmarks without formal semantics or specialized training.

[Beyond Language Modeling: An Exploration of Multimodal Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03276). Training a single model from scratch with mixed text, image, and video data—using semantic representation autoencoders plus MoE—shows joint vision‑language learning without fatal interference, reveals a vision data hunger and scaling asymmetry mitigated by sparsity, and demonstrates that video‑rich multimodal pretraining yields emergent world‑modeling and generation/understanding abilities.

[Semantic Tube Prediction: Beating LLM Data Efficiency with JEPA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22617). Adding an STP auxiliary loss that minimizes components of hidden‑state deviation perpendicular to inferred geodesic trajectories (“semantic tube”) improves signal‑to‑noise ratio, data efficiency, and diversity in autoregressive LLM training compared to next‑token prediction alone.

[Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence](https://www.anthropic.com/research/labor-market-impacts). An observed‑exposure metric combining task‑level theoretical LLM capability with actual Claude usage suggests AI adoption is well below potential; higher‑exposure occupations tend to grow more slowly and include older, female, more‑educated, and higher‑paid workers; and so far there is no clear rise in unemployment, though hiring of 22–25‑year‑olds into exposed jobs appears to have slowed.

[The Spike, the Sparse and the Sink: Anatomy of Massive Activations and Attention Sinks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05498). Massive outlier activations and attention sinks commonly co‑occur in pretrained decoder‑only Transformers due to specific normalization choices, attention‑space dimensionality, and context‑length training; each phenomenon can be independently mitigated without hurting language‑modeling performance.

[Progressive Residual Warmup for Language Model Pretraining](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05369). ProRes gradually scales each layer’s residual from zero to one during training—delaying deeper layers’ contributions so shallow layers stabilize first—improving perplexity and depth scaling across models from 71M to 7B parameters.

[Reasoning Models Struggle to Control their Chains of Thought](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05706). CoT‑Control, a 14,076‑instance benchmark, evaluates how well models follow explicit instructions to modify their chains of thought; most reasoning models struggle with controllability, which varies with model size, reasoning effort, training, and situational‑awareness prompting.
