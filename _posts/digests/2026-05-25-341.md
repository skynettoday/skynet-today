A federal jury in Oakland, California deliberated for less than two hours before unanimously rejecting Elon Musk's $150 billion lawsuit against OpenAI, Sam Altman, Greg Brockman, and Microsoft. Jurors found that Musk had blown past the statute of limitations — filing his 2024 lawsuit over alleged "breach of charitable trust" and "unjust enrichment" despite knowing about OpenAI's for-profit restructuring since at least 2021, a three-year SOL cutoff. Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers dismissed all claims, including those against Microsoft. Musk had sought removal of Altman and Brockman, the unwinding of OpenAI's 2025 for-profit restructuring, and up to $180 billion in "ill-gotten gains" returned to OpenAI's nonprofit. Musk's lead lawyer immediately announced an appeal to the Ninth Circuit, and Musk himself posted — then deleted — a message calling Judge Gonzalez Rogers a "terrible activist Oakland judge."

The three-week trial produced damaging revelations on both sides. Greg Brockman testified that in 2017, Musk pressured him to bring top OpenAI researchers — including Andrej Karpathy, Ilya Sutskever, and Scott Gray — to Tesla's headquarters to assist with Autopilot development without reimbursing OpenAI, a detail one Columbia Law professor called "a bit rich" given Musk's breach-of-charitable-trust claims. Brockman's own journal entries, introduced by Musk's lawyers, showed he had written about being "warm to steal the nonprofit from [Musk]," yet Brockman now holds a $30 billion stake in OpenAI's for-profit while never fulfilling his original $100,000 donation pledge. Sutskever testified that when Musk left OpenAI's board in 2018, Musk declared the company had "0 percent chance of success." With the lawsuit resolved and no remaining legal obstacles, OpenAI moved immediately to file confidentially for an IPO with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, targeting a fall 2026 debut at a potential $1 trillion valuation — the company currently reports $30 billion in annualized revenue but remains deeply unprofitable. SpaceX, which absorbed xAI in February and is valued at $1.25 trillion, is expected to file its own IPO prospectus around the same time.

#### [AI just solved an 80-year-old 'Erdős problem,' and mathematicians are amazed](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ai-just-solved-an-80-year-old-erdos-problem-and-mathematicians-are-amazed/)
Related:
 * [OpenAI claims it solved an 80-year-old math problem — for real this time](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/20/openai-claims-it-solved-an-80-year-old-math-problem-for-real-this-time/)
 * [https://openai.com/index/model-disproves-discrete-geometry-conjecture/](https://openai.com/index/model-disproves-discrete-geometry-conjecture/)
 * [Google Deepmind's AlphaProof Nexus solves decades-old math problems for a few hundred dollars](https://the-decoder.com/google-deepminds-alphaproof-nexus-solves-decades-old-math-problems-for-a-few-hundred-dollars/)

![](https://static.scientificamerican.com/dam/asset/dfbbb93b-a7c7-40f3-b930-0f02f06e4693/GettyImages-2259697316.jpg?m=1779377306.136&w=1200)

On May 20, 2026, OpenAI announced that an internal general-purpose reasoning model disproved a foundational conjecture in discrete geometry first posed by Paul Erdős in 1946: the **unit distance problem**, which asks for the maximum number of point pairs exactly distance 1 apart among n points on a plane. For 80 years, the prevailing belief was that **square-grid constructions** were essentially optimal, yielding at most n^(1+o(1)) unit-distance pairs. By constructing an infinite family of point configurations achieving at least **n^(1+δ)** pairs for a fixed positive constant δ — later refined by Princeton mathematician **Will Sawin** to δ = 0.014 — the AI delivered the first improvement to the lower bound since Erdős's original 1946 result. The technique was notably cross-disciplinary, drawing on **algebraic number theory** — specifically **Gaussian integers**, **infinite class field tower**, and **Golod-Shafarevich theory** — to construct a higher-dimensional lattice with richer symmetries that was then projected ("shadowed") down to 2D. Fields Medal laureate **Timothy Gowers** called it "a milestone in AI mathematics," and mathematicians noted the AI's advantage was its willingness to pursue tedious, seemingly unpromising paths that human experts, largely convinced the conjecture was true, had never attempted.

The achievement comes with important caveats and broader context. External mathematicians like **Daniel Litt** and **Melanie Matchett Wood** emphasized that human cleanup and verification were essential, and that the AI failed to credit similar existing ideas in the literature — which Wood called potential "professional malpractice" if done by a human. Notably, the mathematical tools used were not novel; the AI simply applied them in an unexplored domain, suggesting other "low-hanging fruit" may exist where experts overlooked straightforward approaches. Meanwhile, Google DeepMind's **AlphaProof Nexus** — a competing framework combining **Gemini 3.1 Pro** with **Lean formal verification** and compiler feedback — independently solved 9 out of 353 open Erdős problems and proved 44 OEIS conjectures for just a few hundred dollars per problem, though Terence Tao cautioned that AI's overall success rate on Erdős problems remains around **1–2%**, concentrated on easier tasks. Both systems mark a genuine shift in AI's role in mathematics, but the upper bound of the unit distance problem — **O(n^(4/3))**, established in 1984 — remains unsolved.

#### [Pope Leo Uses First Major Papal Text to Warn About Dangers of AI](https://time.com/article/2026/05/25/pope-leo-encyclical-ai-magnifica-humanitas/)
Related:
 * [https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/26/us/pope-leo-ai-anthropic.html](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/26/us/pope-leo-ai-anthropic.html)
 * [https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/26/technology/pope-leo-ai-religion.html](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/26/technology/pope-leo-ai-religion.html)

Pope Leo XIV released his first encyclical, *Magnifica Humanitas* ("Magnificent Humanity"), on May 25, 2026 — a 42,300-word document formally subtitled "On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence." Deliberately signed on May 15, the 135th anniversary of Pope Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum*, the document draws a direct parallel between the Church's response to the Industrial Revolution and the current AI era. Applying Catholic social principles — subsidiarity, solidarity, the dignity of work, and the common good — to AI, the encyclical warns against "a race for ever more powerful algorithms and larger datasets" driven by geopolitical and commercial dominance. Leo framed the choice facing humanity through two biblical images: the Tower of Babel and the rebuilding of Jerusalem under Nehemiah, representing prideful technocratic power versus human-centered community.

Among its concrete demands are robust legal frameworks, independent oversight, and explicit restrictions on AI in warfare — declaring it "not permissible to entrust lethal decisions" to AI systems and calling the "just war" theory "outdated" given modern technological advances, a direct rebuke of the Trump administration's AI deregulation push. Leo also criticized the concentration of AI power in private hands, stating "a more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few," and issued a special appeal to developers: "every design choice reflects a vision of humanity." Anthropic co-founder Christopher Olah attended the Vatican presentation, where he acknowledged that AI labs "operate inside a set of incentives and constraints that can sometimes conflict with doing the right thing," disclosed that his research team has found structures inside AI models that "mirror results from human neuroscience" and internal states resembling "joy, satisfaction, fear, and grief," and called for more civil society voices to guide AI development. Notably, the encyclical also included a historic papal apology for the Catholic Church's role in slavery — the first time a pope has publicly acknowledged and asked pardon for past papal condoning of colonization and enslavement.

#### [America's dangerous, messy deepfakes crackdown is here](https://www.theverge.com/policy/933518/take-it-down-act-notice-removal-social-media-deepfake)

![](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/STK419_DEEPFAKE_3_CVIRGINIA_C.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C10.732984293194%2C100%2C78.534031413613&w=1200)

Signed by President Trump last May and now fully in force as of May 19th, 2026, the **Take It Down Act** requires online platforms to remove **nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII)** — including AI-generated sexual deepfakes — within **48 hours** or face civil penalties exceeding **$53,000 per violation**. The **FTC**, tasked with enforcement, sent letters to over a dozen major platforms including Amazon, Meta, Google, Apple, TikTok, X, Snapchat, Discord, and Reddit, instructing them to provide easy takedown request processes and remove "known identical copies." Major platforms expressed support and claimed compliance, with Meta stating it had "already been compliant for several months," Snap pointing to proactive nude detection tools, and TikTok citing partnerships with NCMEC and StopNCII.org. In its first year, the law's criminal provision was used in just one conviction — an Ohio man who produced AI deepfakes to harass victims.

Despite broad platform support, experts and civil liberties groups including the **EFF**, **Cato Institute**, and **Cyber Civil Rights Initiative** warn the law risks enabling censorship and over-moderation rather than genuinely protecting victims. Mary Anne Franks of the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative flagged Trump's own State of the Union quip — "I'm going to use that bill for myself" — as a signal the law may be used to settle political scores rather than protect victims, potentially targeting platforms like Wikipedia while giving Trump-friendly platforms a pass. Several notable gaps remain: some of **Grok's** nonconsensual sexualized imagery may not meet the "sexually explicit" threshold, and it's unclear whether AI tools like Grok qualify as "creators" subject to criminal liability. On the detection front, **Google** announced expanded **SynthID** watermark verification coming to Chrome, Search, and Google Lens, while **OpenAI** is partnering with Google to embed both **SynthID** invisible watermarks and **C2PA metadata** in AI-generated images — complementary systems where C2PA provides detailed provenance information and SynthID offers greater durability against manipulation like screenshots and resizing.
